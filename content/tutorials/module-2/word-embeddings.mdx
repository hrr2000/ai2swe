# Semantic Hash Codes

In the previous lesson, we saw that embeddings are arrays of numbers. But where do these numbers come from? How did we learn that "King" is similar to "Queen"?

Before the giant LLMs of 2026, we used two revolutionary algorithms: **Word2Vec** and **GloVe**.

## Word2Vec: You are defined by your neighbors

The core idea of Word2Vec is simple: **A word's meaning is determined by the words that frequently appear around it.**

If you see the word "Gigabyte" in 10,000 documents, it's almost always surrounded by "RAM," "Storage," "Computer," and "Transfer."  The algorithm looks at these neighbors and builds a vector that clusters "Gigabyte" near the vectors for its neighbors.

### The "Prediction" Game
Word2Vec trains by playing a game:
- **Skip-gram**: Given a word, predict its neighbors.
- **CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)**: Given some neighbors, predict the missing word.

By failing and succeeding millions of times, the model builds a map where "Java" is near "Python" because they share similar "neighbor contexts" (like "code," "programming," "developer").

## GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation

While Word2Vec looks at local neighbors, **GloVe** looks at the entire dataset at once. It builds a massive "Co-occurrence Matrix." 

Think of it like a giant spreadsheet where every row and column is a word in the dictionary. Each cell records how many times those two words appeared together in the same document.

GloVe then uses matrix factorization to squish this giant sparse spreadsheet into small, dense vectors (like 300 dimensions).

## The Legacy

Today, we use much more advanced "Transformer-based" embeddings, but modern AI still stands on the shoulders of these two giants. 

> [!NOTE]
> The biggest limitation of Word2Vec and GloVe? They are **Stateless**. The word "Bank" has the same vector whether you are "sitting on a river bank" or "withdrawing money from a bank." Modern AI fixed this with *Contextual* embeddings.
