## Before everything was a Transformer

Today you often call an API and get **contextual** embeddings (the same word gets different vectors depending on the sentence). In the 2010s, **static word vectors** were the breakthrough: one fixed vector per **word type** in the vocabulary, learned from billions of tokens of running text.

Two families dominated: **Word2Vec** (2013) and **GloVe** (2014). They are still useful pedagogically — they make the **distributional hypothesis** concrete.

> “You shall know a word by the company it keeps.” — J.R. Firth (1957), paraphrased

## Word2Vec: predict neighbors, learn rows

Word2Vec trains a shallow network to play a **self-supervised** game on raw text:

- **Skip-gram** — given a center word, predict surrounding context words within a window.
- **CBOW** — given context words, predict the center word.

Wrong predictions produce loss; gradients update **two embedding tables** (center and context). After training, you typically drop the context side and keep the **center embeddings** as your word vectors.

Why it works: words with interchangeable contexts (“Python” near “code”, “Java” near “code”) get nudged toward similar rows in the embedding matrix.

## GloVe: count first, factorize second

**GloVe (Global Vectors)** constructs a huge **co-occurrence matrix** `X_ij` — how often word `i` appears near word `j` across the corpus — then learns low-rank vectors `w_i` and `w_j` so that dot products `w_i · w_j` approximate log counts (with bias terms).

You can think of it as **matrix factorization** on a log-smoothed PMI-like objective: global statistics first, then compression into 100–300 dimensions.

## What you get in practice

- **Linear structure** — famous `king − man + woman ≈ queen` demos worked because the geometry encoded analogy directions.
- **Tiny models** — a few hundred MB of vectors, trivial by LLM standards.

## Limitations (why we moved on)

Static embeddings conflate senses: **“bank”** (river) vs **“bank”** (finance) share one vector. Modern Transformers produce **contextual** representations — the effective embedding is a function of the whole sentence.

<Callout type="insight">
Word2Vec/GloVe teach you what **embedding rows** are. Transformers teach you what **dynamic, layered representations** are. The scaling story changed; the geometric intuition did not.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- **Word2Vec** learns vectors by **predicting local context** (skip-gram / CBOW).
- **GloVe** learns vectors by **factorizing global co-occurrence** statistics.
- Both yield **static** word vectors — powerful history, but replaced by contextual models for ambiguous language.
