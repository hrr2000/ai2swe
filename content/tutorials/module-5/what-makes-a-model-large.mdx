## Three axes people blur together

1. **Parameters (N_params)** — count of weights in θ. Marketing “**70B**” refers to this.
2. **Training tokens** — how many tokens the optimizer saw. Chinchilla scaling laws emphasize **balancing** params and data.
3. **Inference compute** — depends on architecture, context length, batch, precision, and KV cache — not captured by parameter count alone.

## Memory intuition

Rough **weight-only** model RAM at FP16: `2 bytes × N_params`. **7B** ≈ 14 GB before optimizer states, activations, or KV cache. Quantization (4-bit) shrinks footprint for inference.

## Why bigger helps — until it doesn’t

More parameters → lower training loss ceilings on fixed data **if** you also scale data and regularization well. Diminishing returns + **inference costs** push teams toward **Mixture-of-Experts** and **distillation** for production.

<Callout type="tip">
Compare models at **similar task suites** (your eval harness), not leaderboard trivia. A smaller model + RAG often beats a larger model alone on **grounded** QA.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- **Billions of parameters** ≈ model “width/depth capacity.”
- **Training tokens** and **compute** complete the scaling picture.
- Deployment cares about **VRAM, latency, and $/query** — derive from profiling, not parameter count alone.
