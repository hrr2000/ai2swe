## What it controls

Gradient descent uses:

```text
θ ← θ − η · ∇L(θ)
```

**η (learning rate)** scales how far you move opposite the gradient. Backprop only tells you **direction** and **relative steepness**; η decides how aggressively you trust that signal on this step.

<AnalogyBox
  code="PID controller gain — higher gain reacts faster but oscillates"
  ai="Learning rate — larger steps converge faster until they diverge or chatter"
/>

## Failure modes

| Symptom | Likely cause |
| --- | --- |
| Loss **NaNs** or explodes | η too large, bad init, or unstabilized mixed precision |
| Loss **crawls** | η too small, or gradients vanishing elsewhere |
| Loss **bounces** | η too large on a noisy mini-batch landscape |

## Schedules and warmup

**Warmup** — start with a tiny η, ramp to base over a few hundred steps — stabilizes Transformer training when gradients are spiky early.

**Cosine decay** — smoothly reduce η so the optimizer takes **large exploratory steps early** and **tiny polishing steps late**.

**One-cycle / restarts** — periodic η spikes can help escape flat regions (less common in LLM pretrain, more in vision sweeps).

<Callout type="tip">
When you **double batch size**, some teams scale η linearly (Goyal et al. style heuristics) up to a point. Always verify on a **dev split** — there is no law of physics here, only empirical stability.
</Callout>

## AdamW still has a “learning rate”

Adaptive optimizers rescale per-parameter updates, but the **global LR** still sets overall step magnitude and interacts with **weight decay**. Fine-tuning LLMs often uses surprisingly small peak LRs (e.g. `1e-5` to `3e-4` range) compared to classical vision nets — consult recipes for your base checkpoint.

---

## Key takeaways

- **η** scales all updates — first knob when loss is unstable.
- Use **schedules + warmup** for deep Transformers and large batches.
- Treat published recipes as **starting points**; your data and batch size change the optimum.
