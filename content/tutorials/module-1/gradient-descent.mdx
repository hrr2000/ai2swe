## The update rule

Given parameters `θ` and loss `L(θ)`, backprop supplies the gradient `∇L(θ)`. **Gradient descent** steps opposite that direction:

```text
θ_{t+1} = θ_t − η ∇L(θ_t)
```

`η` (eta) is the **learning rate** — step size. Too large: oscillation or divergence. Too small: progress measured in geological time.

## Intuition on a bowl

For convex quadratic bowls, with a well-chosen `η`, iterates spiral cleanly to the minimum. Real neural loss surfaces are **non-convex**, riddled with saddle points and flat regions — yet first-order methods work surprisingly well in practice when paired with **momentum**, **batch noise**, and **good initialization** (often from pretraining).

## Stochasticity: mini-batches

Full **batch** gradient descent uses the entire dataset each step — expensive. **Stochastic** GD uses one example; noisy but fast. **Mini-batch** GD averages gradients over `B` examples — the default compromise. That noise can even help **escape sharp minima**.

## AdamW and friends

Plain SGD is still used, but **Adam / AdamW** (adaptive per-parameter learning rates with momentum) dominate transformer fine-tuning defaults. You still tune a global LR and weight decay; the optimizer handles per-parameter scaling of updates.

<Callout type="warning">
There is no universal LR. Changes in batch size, precision (fp16/bf16), depth, and loss scaling all shift stability. Treat LR schedules like **capacity planning** — log sweeps, not guesses.
</Callout>

## When gradient descent is not the whole story

RL, discrete search, and some discrete structured prediction problems need other tools. But for differentiable deep nets, **autograd + AdamW + cosine schedule** is the modern bread and butter.

---

## Key takeaways

- **Gradient descent** reduces loss by stepping opposite `∇L`.
- **Learning rate** is the primary stability knob; **batch size** trades noise vs cost.
- Advanced optimizers adapt step sizes, but they do not remove the need for **careful tuning** and **evaluation**.
