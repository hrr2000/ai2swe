## Mechanism

Each token embedding hits a **router** (linear + softmax) producing scores over `E` experts. Only **top-k** experts run their feed-forward blocks; outputs are **weighted summed**.

Result: **total parameter count** explodes (good for knowledge capacity) while **per-token compute** stays closer to dense models of smaller width.

<AnalogyBox
  code="API gateway sending requests to sharded services based on headers"
  ai="Router picks specialists — not every expert runs on every token"
/>

## Engineering implications

- **Memory** still stores all experts — need multi-GPU **tensor parallelism** or **offload**.
- **Load balancing** — routers can collapse to **expert collapse** if training imbalanced; auxiliary losses encourage uniform use.
- **Latency variance** — hot experts become bottlenecks without careful scheduling.

<Callout type="tip">
When benchmarking MoE APIs, test **diverse prompts** — worst-case latency spikes when rare expert combos activate under cold cache conditions.
</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- MoE decouples **parameter capacity** from **per-token FLOPs**.
- Serving MoE is a **systems problem** — not just a graph rewrite.
- Great for **frontier knowledge breadth** at similar active compute.
